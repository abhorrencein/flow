Section 01

The Signal

In January 2026, the Economic Survey 2025-26 delivered the number everyone expected: 7.4% real GDP growth for FY26. The fastest among major economies. The Survey projected potential growth upgraded to 7%. Within weeks, MoSPI's GDP base year revision pushed the figure to 7.6% under the new 2022-23 series.

The international institutions largely concurred. The IMF's 2025 Article IV pegged growth at 6.6%, noting continued momentum alongside tariff headwinds. The ADB settled on 6.5%. India was growing, and growing fast.

But the number that should have led every front page sat quietly in the World Bank's development indicators: India's manufacturing value added as a share of GDP stood at 12.53% in 2024 — the lowest since the dataset began in 1960. Lower than the licence raj. Moving in the opposite direction of the government's 25% target.

This is the paradox that defines India's current moment: a growth engine running hotter than any comparable economy, producing wealth concentrating faster than at any point in a century, and building an industrial base that is — by its own chosen metric — shrinking as a share of the whole.

Section 02

The Data

The PLI scheme, launched in 2020 with ₹1.97 lakh crore across 14 sectors, has delivered real results: ₹1.76 lakh crore in investments, ₹16.5 lakh crore in production, electronics output up 146%. These numbers are not fiction.

But the scheme promised 60 lakh jobs over five years. It has created 12 lakh — one-fifth of the target. Manufacturing's employment share has inched to just 12.1%. India is adding factory output without proportionally adding factory workers.

The Female LFPR Illusion

The headline improvement is dramatic: from 23.3% in 2017-18 to 40.0% in 2025. But as an EPW analysis from June 2025 documents, this surge is largely driven by rural women entering own-account and unpaid self-employment in agriculture.

The share of women in regular salaried jobs has actually declined. Urban female LFPR was just 25.3% in December 2025. The World Inequality Report 2026 records India's female share of labour income at 15.7% — unchanged in a decade.

Section 03

The Pattern

In March 2024, the World Inequality Lab released a finding that reframed India's growth debate. Authored by Bharti, Chancel, Piketty, and Somanchi: the top 1% of earners now capture 22.6% of pre-tax national income. The top 1% control over 40% of total wealth. Both at their highest since 1922.

The top 10% receive 58% of national income. The bottom 50% receive 15%. The top 10% share rose from 30% in 1982 to nearly 58% by 2023, driven by the top 1%, whose share grew from 6.1% to 22.6%. The World Inequality Report 2026, released December 2025, confirmed this gap has shown no signs of narrowing.

What makes this structurally significant — not merely politically uncomfortable — is its relationship to the growth model. Services generate ~55% of GDP but employ relatively fewer per unit of output. Agriculture employs 43% but contributes under 18% of GVA. Manufacturing sits between, contributing 12–17% of GDP depending on methodology, but only 12.1% of formal employment.

Section 04

What This Means

India's growth is real. The DPI infrastructure — Aadhaar, UPI, GSTN — is genuinely world-class. Manufacturing FDI rose 18% in FY2024-25. The PLI scheme has delivered in electronics and pharma. Inflation is contained at 2.8%. The fiscal deficit is being managed.

The question is not whether India is growing. The question is whether this model — services-heavy, capital-intensive, concentrated at the top — can get 1.4 billion people through the door. The data suggests it cannot, at least not in its current form.

Three structural constraints stand out. First, manufacturing employment has decoupled from output. PLI drives capital investment, but jobs-per-crore-invested falls as firms automate. The 12 lakh jobs against a 60 lakh target is not delivery failure — it is modern manufacturing's structural reality.

Second, the female LFPR recovery is largely fictive in quality terms. The increase reflects rural women being counted as workers in own-account agriculture and unpaid family enterprise — not women entering formal, remunerative employment.

Third, income concentration is a demand constraint. When the top 10% captures 58% of national income and the bottom 50% captures 15%, consumer demand is structurally narrow. This explains volume stagnation in mass-market FMCG, the persistent K-shaped recovery, and why luxury booms while the base stays flat.

India's velvet rope is not a conspiracy. It is a structure — built from specific choices about which sectors to incentivise, which metrics to optimise, and which populations to count. The first step to dismantling it is to measure it honestly.

Methodology & Limitations

What this analysis can and cannot say

This story draws on 22 official and academic sources: government statistical publications (MoF Economic Survey, PLFS, PIB/DPIIT dashboards), multilateral assessments (IMF Article IV, World Bank CEM, OECD, UNCTAD, ADB), RBI handbooks, NITI Aayog working papers, and the World Inequality Lab's distributional national accounts.

GDP figures vary by base year and methodology. We report ranges (6.5–7.6%) and name the specific source for each figure. Manufacturing GDP share varies between 12.53% (World Bank narrow ISIC) and ~17% (CII/national accounts broader definition). Both are reported.

PLFS data underwent a methodological shift in January 2025 (July-June → January-December cycle). Pre- and post-2025 data are not directly comparable. Monthly CWS and annual usual-status data measure different constructs — we report both, clearly labelled.

World Inequality Lab estimates combine income tax tabulations, national accounts, rich lists, and survey data via distributional national accounts methodology. These are estimates, not administrative counts. Significant uncertainty exists in bottom-half income estimation.

This analysis does not claim India's growth is illusory or that PLI has failed. It claims — and the data supports — that growth benefits are highly concentrated, manufacturing employment has not scaled with output, and headline labour improvements conceal significant quality gaps.
